I work at the intersection of data science, engineering, and social research, with a focus on how technical systems are designed, evaluated, and used in practice.

My early work focused on energy planning. In my master's thesis, I built an optimization model for Turkey's electricity mix that considered cost and sustainability indicators. The results pointed strongly toward hydropower: economically attractive and comparatively low in emissions on paper.

What complicated that "clean" result was everything the model could not see. At the time, dams were contested in Turkey because of submergence, ecological damage, and disruption of livelihoods. I conducted field visits and began reading across Science and Technology Studies (STS), trying to understand how technical decisions become social realities, and why a solution can be rational in one frame and destructive in another.

That interest became my PhD direction. I moved into sociology to study large technical systems in practice: how plans travel, where they break, and how people negotiate the consequences. Around the same time, I co-founded STS Istanbul ([stsistanbul.org](https://stsistanbul.org)) to create a space for exactly these cross-disciplinary conversations.

After my PhD, I worked as a Wissenschaftliche Mitarbeiterin at TU Berlin. While I was finishing my research project, the rise of large language models made something very clear to me: writing code is not the hard part anymore. Many tools can generate decent solutions. What still makes a difference is problem framing, knowing what to ask, what "good" looks like, and how to judge results when there are trade-offs.

That is the part I bring. I am used to working without a clean dataset or a tidy question. I know how to form hypotheses, define evaluation criteria, and keep multiple perspectives in view. I then translate that into implementation: applied ML projects, NLP pipelines, and systems that are tested against real constraints.

I am currently looking for roles in data science or AI engineering where this mix is useful: building and shipping models, but also thinking clearly about assumptions, evaluation, and impact.
